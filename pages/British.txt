American (AmE) and British (BrE) English have distinct pronunciation patterns, often with BrE vowels sounding longer/more rounded (like in 'lot' or 'bath'), different stress in words like "advertisement," and varied 'R' sounds (BrE often dropping 'R' after vowels), leading to noticeable differences in words such as 'schedule' (AmE: SKED-yool; BrE: SHED-yool) or 'either' (AmE: EE-thur; BrE: EYE-thur), showing unique vowel shifts and rhythm
. 
Here's a list highlighting some common words with notable pronunciation distinctions:
Vowel Differences (The /ɑ/ vs. /ɒ/ sound)

    Lot: AmE uses a lower, open /ɑ/ (LAHT), while BrE uses a rounded /ɒ/ (LOT).
    Bath, Grass: AmE often uses /æ/ (BATH, GRASS); BrE uses a longer /ɑː/ (BAH-th, GRAH-ss).
    Tomato: AmE: toh-MAY-toh; BrE: toh-MAH-toh (longer 'a' sound). 

Consonant & 'R' Sound Differences

    Schedule: AmE: SKED-yool; BrE: SHED-yool (the 's' becomes 'sh').
    Water: AmE often pronounces the 't' as a 'd' sound (WA-der); BrE pronounces it more clearly (WA-tuh).
    Car: AmE: KAR; BrE: KAH (dropping the 'r' sound unless followed by a vowel). 

Stress & Vowel Shifts

    Advertisement: AmE: ad-ver-TIS-ment; BrE: ad-VER-tis-ment (stress on the second syllable in BrE).
    Either: AmE: EE-thur; BrE: EYE-thuh (different initial vowel).
    Garage: AmE: guh-RAHJ; BrE: GAR-idge (different vowel & 'g' sound). 

Key Takeaway: British English often features longer, more rounded vowels and a
non-rhotic 'r' (not pronouncing 'r' at the end of words), while American English
tends to have shorter vowels and a rhotic 'r' (pronouncing 'r' everywhere), plus
different stress patterns on many words.
