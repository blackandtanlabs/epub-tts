French speakers pronounce English words by adding French sounds
(like rolling 'R's, soft 'T's, silent 'H's, vowel shifts like 'ship' to 'sheep'), often making them sound softer, more melodic, and sometimes changing vowel lengths, leading to 'Table' sounding like 'Tah-bluh', 'Hotel' ignoring the 'H', and 'Three' becoming 'Sree' or 'Tree' with a softer 'Th'. 
Here's a list of common English words with typical French pronunciations (approximations):
Vowel & Consonant Shifts

    Table (English: Tay-bull) -> (French: Tah-bluh)
    Radio (English: Ray-dee-oh) -> (French: Ra-dee-oh)
    Ship (English: Ship) -> (French: Sheep) (ih becomes ee)
    Kid (English: Kid) -> (French: Keed) (ih becomes ee)
    Cart (English: Kart) -> (French: Caht) (ar becomes ah)
    Man (English: Man) -> (French: Mahn) (nasal 'an') 

Silent Letters & 'H'

    Hotel (English: Hoh-tel) -> (French: O-tel) (H is silent)
    Hungry (English: Hun-gree) -> (French: On-gree) (H often dropped)
    Although (English: Awl-thoh) -> (French: Ohl-doh) (Th becomes D/Z) 

The 'TH' Sound

    Three (English: Three) -> (French: Sree or Tree) (TH becomes S or T)
    Clothes (English: Klohz) -> (French: Klohz or Klohz-ez) (Voiced TH is tricky) 

Difficult Sounds

    Rural (English: Roo-rul) -> (French: Roo-ral or Roo-rul) (French 'R' is guttural, 'L' is softer)
    Maneuver (English: Ma-noo-ver) -> (French: Ma-nu-ver) (Ends with soft 'er') 

Common Anglicisms (English words used in French)

    OK: Often pronounced as written (Oh-Kay).
    Cool: Pronounced as written (Kool).
    Weekend: Often heard as "Le Weekend" (Luh Wee-kend).
    Email: Heard as "Un Mail" (Uh Mah-eel) or "Un E-mail" (Uh-ee-mail).
