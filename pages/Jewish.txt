Variations in the pronunciation of English words by Jewish speakers are primarily due to the influence of Hebrew and Yiddish phonology, rather than slang or substitution
. Different Jewish communities (e.g., Ashkenazi, Sephardic, Israeli) may exhibit distinct accents.
Here are some common English pronunciation differences influenced by the phonetic features of Hebrew and Yiddish:

    "W" sound as "V": Hebrew does not have a "w" sound (the phoneme /w/). As a result, some Hebrew speakers may pronounce English words containing "w" with a "v" sound (the phoneme /v/).
        General English: "White" (/wa…™t/), "water" (/ňąw…Ēňźt…ôr/)
        Jewish English: "Vite" (/va…™t/), "vater" (/ňąv…Ēňźt…ôr/)
    "Th" sounds as "T" or "D": The "voiceless th" (/őł/ as in "think") and the "voiced th" (/√į/ as in "this") sounds do not exist in Hebrew. Hebrew speakers often substitute these sounds with "t" (/t/) or "d" (/d/) respectively.
        General English: "Think" (/őł…™Ňčk/), "math" (/m√¶őł/), "this" (/√į…™s/), "brother" (/ňąbr Ć√į…ôr/)
        Jewish English: "Tink" (/t…™Ňčk/), "mat" (/m√¶t/), "dis" (/d…™s/), "broder" (/ňąbr Ćd…ôr/)
    Vowel Pronunciation (The "Thought" Vowel): Some Jewish English speakers, particularly those with a strong New York City linguistic background, tend to raise the "thought" vowel (the /…Ēňź/ sound in words like thought, coffee, dog). This can make words like "coffee" sound more like "cawfee" or even "cooffee" to some listeners.
        General English: "Dog" (/d…Ēňźg/), "coffee" (/ňąk…ífiňź/)
        Jewish English (NYC influence): "Daug" (/d…Ē…ôg/ or /d ä…ôg/), "cawfee" (/ňąk…Ē…ôfiňź/)
    "H" sound variation: The letter 'H' may sometimes be pronounced as an 'A' sound in certain contexts, particularly when influenced by a specific Israeli accent where the 'Hei' (◊Ē) at the end of a word is often silent in modern Hebrew.
        General English: "How" (/ha ä/)
        Jewish English (Israeli influence): "Ow" (/a ä/)
    "R" sound: The "r" sound in Hebrew (Resh) is often a guttural or trilled 'r', similar to French or German, which is different from the standard American English retroflex "r" sound. This can affect the pronunciation of "r" in many common English words.
        General English: "Run" (/r Ćn/)
        Jewish English (Israeli influence): A 'ch' (guttural) or French-like 'r' sound at the beginning of the word.
    Stress Patterns: Hebrew uses a more syllable-based rhythm in speech, whereas English is stress-based. Hebrew speakers might place stress on a different syllable than a native English speaker would, altering the sound of the word.
        General English: "TAble" (stress on the first syllable), "aSIDE" (stress on the second syllable)
        Jewish English (varied): May exhibit different, less predictable stress patterns.

These are generalized observations based on common linguistic influences and do
not apply to all Jewish individuals, many of whom speak English with no
discernible regional Jewish accent.
