Italian speakers often pronounce English words by applying Italian phonetics, emphasizing vowels, adding extra vowels to end consonants (like "it" becoming "it-ah"), softening 'th' sounds to 'd' or 't', and struggling with English diphthongs (like "go" sounding closer to "goh-oh") or short 'i' sounds (like 'ship' becoming 'sheep'), leading to clearer, vowel-heavy, but sometimes slightly slower pronunciations, as they don't substitute slang but adapt sounds to their native system
. 
Here's a breakdown with examples:
Vowel Sounds & Endings:

    "It": Pronounced closer to ee-t (with a slightly longer 'ee').
    "But": Sounds like boot or boo-t (short 'u' becomes longer).
    "Ship": Might sound like sheep (short 'i' becomes long 'ee').
    "Go" / "No": Often pronounced with a more distinct, drawn-out vowel, like goh-oh or noh-oh (Italian doesn't have the English diphthong 'oh').
    "Meet": Sounds like meh-et or mee-et (vowels are often clearer). 

Consonant Shifts:

    "The" / "This": 'Th' often becomes 'd' (e.g., deh) or 't' (e.g., teh), or even 'z'.
    "Think": 'Th' becomes 't' (e.g., tink).
    "Water": Often pronounced with a clearer 't' sound, closer to wah-ter. 

Common Pitfalls:

    Double Consonants: Italian has no double consonants in the English sense, so they might be pronounced lightly or as single sounds, or even add a vowel.
    'H' at Start: Sometimes the 'h' in words like 'house' might be slightly softer or omitted if they're not used to it. 

Example Words:

    "Water": Wah-ter (clearer 't').
    "Hello": Heh-loh (clearer 'e' and 'o').
    "Thank you": Tahnk yoo (clear 'a' and 'u').
    "Please": Plee-z (short 'ee', softer 's').
    "Sorry": Soh-ree (clearer vowels). 

They generally don't use slang substitutes but apply their native Italian sound
system (phonetics) to English words, making them sound melodic and vowel-rich
but with distinct pronunciation differences.
