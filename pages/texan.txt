A Texan accent blends Southern/South Midland sounds, featuring
elongated 'R's (barn), the 'pin/pen' vowel merger (sounding same), flattened 'i's (like "ah" in "nice"), dropping 'g's (goin'), and unique vocabulary like "y'all," but the key is a slower pace with clear, slightly nasal vowel sounds and a rolled 'R' for emphasis, not really slang, but a distinct delivery. 
Here's how common words shift, focusing on pronunciation shifts without slang:

    Water: "Waw-ter" (more like 'aw' sound).
    Fire: "Fai-yer" (adding a slight 'y' sound).
    Time: "Tahm" (long 'i' becomes 'ah').
    Nice: "Nah-ice" (similar to 'time').
    Going: "Goin'" (dropped 'g').
    Barn: "Baa-rn" (long, emphasized 'R').
    Pen/Pin: Often sound identical ("P'n").
    Home: "Hohm" (like 'oh' in 'boat').
    You all: "Y'all" (essential Texan usage). 

Key Sounds:

    Vowels: Often draw out or flatten vowels (e.g., "I" as "ah").
    'R's: Pronounced more clearly, often adding emphasis or a slight roll.
    Vocal Fry/Nasality: A slight nasal quality can be present. 

Example: Instead of "I'm going to the store," it might sound like "Ahm goin' ta
the stohr" (with a rolled 'R' on store).
