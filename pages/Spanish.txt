Spanish speakers often pronounce English words using Spanish phonetic rules, as English has a much larger inventory of vowel and consonant sounds (around 19 vowels and 25 consonants) compared to Spanish (five pure vowels and 16-20 consonants)
.
Here is a list of common English words and how a native Spanish speaker might pronounce them, reflecting common phonological transfers:
Vowels
Spanish speakers may confuse English's many short and long vowel sounds or the unstressed schwa sound (uh).
English Word (Correct Pronunciation)	Spanish Speaker Pronunciation (Phonetic Adaptation)	Reason for Difference
Ship (/ʃɪp/)	Sheep (/ʃiːp/) or Sip (/sɪp/)	Spanish has no short "i" (/ɪ/) or "sh" (/ʃ/) sound.
Live (/lɪv/)	Leave (/liːv/)	The short "i" in English is often rendered as a long "e" sound.
Pull (/pʊl/)	Pool (/puːl/)	Spanish lacks the short "u" sound (/ʊ/).
Cat (/kæt/)	Cut (/kʌt/) or Cot (/kɑːt/)	English sounds like "a" in "cat" (/æ/) or "u" in "cut" (/ʌ/) don't exist in Spanish.
But (/bʌt/)	Bat (/bæt/)	Confusion between the short "u" (/ʌ/) and "a" (/æ/) sounds.
Consonants
Certain consonant sounds or clusters in English do not exist in Spanish, leading to substitutions.
English Word (Correct Pronunciation)	Spanish Speaker Pronunciation (Phonetic Adaptation)	Reason for Difference
Very (/ˈvɛri/)	Berry (/ˈbɛri/)	In Spanish, the "b" and "v" sounds are pronounced almost identically, as a soft "b" sound.
Think (/θɪŋk/)	Tink (/tɪŋk/) or Sink (/sɪŋk/)	The "th" (/θ/) sound does not exist in Spanish and is often replaced with "t" or "s".
This (/ðɪs/)	Dis (/dɪs/)	The voiced "th" (/ð/) is typically replaced with a "d" sound.
Jail (/dʒeɪl/)	Yale (/jeɪl/)	The "j" sound (/dʒ/) can be confused with the English "y" sound (/j/).
Shampoo (/ʃæmˈpuː/)	Champoo (/tʃæmˈpuː/)	The "sh" sound (/ʃ/) doesn't exist in Spanish and is often pronounced as "ch" (/tʃ/).
Stop (/stɒp/)	E-stop (/ɛstɒp/)	Spanish words never start with an "s" followed by a consonant (like st or sp), so an "e" sound is often added.
Happy (/ˈhæpi/)	Appy (/ˈæpi/) or (Overly aspirated "h")	The letter "h" is usually silent in Spanish, so the sound may be omitted or over-pronounced.
Time (/taɪm/)	Tine (/taɪn/)	Words in Spanish rarely end in an "m" sound; "n" is more common.
Other Pronunciation Features

    Word Stress: Spanish has a more regular word stress system. Spanish speakers may apply that rhythm to English words, stressing every syllable more evenly or placing stress on different syllables than a native English speaker would.
    Intonation: Spanish is a "syllable-timed" language, meaning each syllable
    has roughly the same duration. English is "stress-timed," with varied
    intonation and reduced unstressed syllables. This can make a Spanish
    speaker's English sound flatter in pitch.
