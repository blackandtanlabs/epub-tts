Many common English words are pronounced differently by Southern U.S. speakers due to a distinct set of
vowel shifts and a tendency to elongate or "drawl" vowels, often making single-syllable words sound like two.
Below is a list of common words, with their typical General American (GA) pronunciation contrasted with a common Southern American English (SAE) pronunciation (approximated non-technically), while avoiding slang or substitute words.

    "A":
        GA: "Ay"
        SAE: "Uh" or "Ah"
    Aunt:
        GA: "Ant" (rhymes with pant)
        SAE: "Ahnt" (rhymes with haunt) or "Aint"
    Buy/By:
        GA: "Bigh" (long 'i' sound)
        SAE: "Bah" (longer "ah" sound)
    Can't:
        GA: "Cant" (short 'a')
        SAE: "Caint" (long 'a' then short 'i' sound, sometimes with a drawl, almost "cay-uhnt")
    Car:
        GA: "Car" (hard 'r')
        SAE: "Caa" (softer 'r' or non-rhoticity in some older/rural dialects)
    Fire:
        GA: "Fiy-er" (two syllables)
        SAE: "Faar" or "Fahr" (often one syllable, the glide is deleted)
    Get:
        GA: "Geht"
        SAE: "Giht"
    Going (as in "going to"):
        GA: "Go-ing" or "Go-eeng"
        SAE: "Goin'" or "Gunna" (the final 'g' is often dropped, or "going to" becomes "gonna")
    Oil:
        GA: "Oy-ill"
        SAE: "Oll" (one syllable)
    On:
        GA: "On" (short 'o')
        SAE: "Own" (long 'o' sound)
    Pen (writing instrument) / Pin (fastener):
        GA: "Pehn" / "Pihn" (distinct vowel sounds)
        SAE: "Pihn" for both words (the vowel sounds in these words are merged, a key feature of the accent)
    Tire:
        GA: "Tiy-er" (two syllables)
        SAE: "Taar" or "Tahr" (often one syllable, the glide is deleted)
    Wash:
        GA: "Wash"
        SAE: "Warsh" (an 'r' sound is often inserted)
    Windows:
        GA: "Win-dohs"
        SAE: "Win-ders" (an 'r' sound is often inserted)
    You all:
        GA: "You all" (two words)
        SAE: "Y'all" (a single contraction used as the second-person plural
        pronoun)
